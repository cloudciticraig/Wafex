The Musson Family · Est. 1991

Two suitcases, one recession, and a flower called wax.

Wafex has only ever had one owner: the family that founded it. This is the story of Craig and Michelle Musson, and the wildflower business they grew from nothing on the other side of the world.

The Founder

Craig Musson

Craig grew up around flowers. In Zimbabwe he worked as a chrysanthemum grower alongside Peter, his father-in-law — the man who first taught him the discipline of a flower crop.

In 1991, with political tension rising at home, Craig and his wife Michelle emigrated to Perth, Western Australia. They arrived into the teeth of a recession. Michelle found work as a primary school teacher; Craig took his first Australian job picking roses on a Swan Valley farm — and was made redundant on Christmas Eve as the downturn bit.

Unemployed, newly arrived and, in his own telling, wondering whether the move had been a mistake, Craig backed himself on the one thing he knew: flowers. Geraldton waxflower — a Western Australian native — was finding fans overseas, and Craig saw an export business where others saw a roadside shrub. Wafex was born.

He has since told that whole story himself, with typical frankness, in his book Life, Lessons and Learnings — a founder's account of building a global business on optimism, hard work and a refusal to let the fear of failure outweigh new opportunity.

The Mussons — a family business from the very first box.

Three Decades of Growth

From one box of wax to the world.

1991

A new start in Perth

Craig and Michelle Musson leave Zimbabwe for Western Australia. Craig picks roses in the Swan Valley until the recession takes his job on Christmas Eve. Within months, he starts exporting Geraldton waxflower — and Wafex begins.

1990s

Natives out, roses in

The early model is simple and bold: ship Australian native wildflowers to the world, and bring premium imported roses home. Packing facilities open in Perth and later Melbourne.

2008

Helix™ — breeding a better waxflower

Wild waxflower genetics were limited: three colours, short seasons, low yields. Wafex launches Helix in partnership with Perth's Kings Park Botanic Garden to breed superior waxflower and boronia — ultimately stretching the season from three months to eight and adding a rainbow of new colours.

2013

Wafex USA

Craig partners with Steve Dionne to open in California, redefining Australian and African wildflowers for the North American market — with exclusive Helix™ waxflower and Ayoba™ protea programs.

2010s

Africa and the Andes

Offices open in Nairobi, Kenya and Quito, Ecuador, putting Wafex people at the source of the world's best summer flowers and high-altitude roses.

Today

Fully vertically integrated — still family-owned

Breeding, farming, exporting, importing, wholesaling, supermarket bouquets and online retail: over 200 staff, 500+ growers, 300 customers and 26 countries. And at the top of it all, the same family that started with two suitcases in 1991.

The Wafex Way

Values you can't outsource.

The flower trade runs on relationships, weather and trust. These are the principles the Mussons have run the business on since day one.

  • Handshakes over contracts

    We work with people we like, who like working with us. Growers and customers become friends; friends become family.

  • Grower-first thinking

    Most of our 500+ suppliers are hard-working family farms. Fair dealing, honest grading and prompt payment keep them growing with us for decades.

  • Freshness is a deadline

    Cold chains, quality assurance and thirty years of logistics craft — because a flower's clock starts ticking at harvest.

  • Never stop breeding

    From Helix waxflower to new protea lines, we invest export earnings back into Australian plant breeding — chasing flowers no one has grown before.
